// CHALLENGES

The Limits of Traditional OCR

[CHALLENGE_LOG_01]

Standard banknote scanners and OCR systems only work if the bills are clean, flat, and fully visible. Our project is designed for the real world: we train our model to recognize currency even when bills are partially covered, stacked on top of each other, or tilted at weird camera angles.

Clean Scan Baseline

Traditional OCR expects flat, isolated, and fully visible banknotes.

01. PARTIAL VISIBILITY

Training the model to recognize a banknote from isolated fragments—like a portrait crop, color strip, or security band—instead of requiring the entire note.

// CLUE DETECTION

02. BILL OVERLAP

Teaching the model to segment overlapping boundaries and count individual bills correctly, preventing them from merging into a single box.

03. CAMERA ANGLES

Methodology

// STEP 01 // DATA SCRAPING & AUDIT

1. Building the Real-World Foundation

Standard currency OCR projects fail because real Cambodian Riel (KHR) and USD banknote photos are scarce, badly cropped, or mislabeled. We scoured the internet to compile every available source across Roboflow and Hugging Face.

Through a rigorous manual audit, we cleaned up bounding boxes, corrected denomination labels, and removed duplicates to assemble a clean baseline dataset.

AUDIT REPORT:

➔ 3,420 verified real-world banknote photos compiled

➔ 100% label and coordinate verification

➔ Corrected major schema issues (like missing USD 1/20/50 classes in public sets)

// STEP 02 // BASELINE MODEL

2. Establish Baseline & Weak Spots

We trained our custom baseline model exclusively on our 3,420 scoured and audited real-world photos. This model acts as our performance floor.

Evaluating this baseline model on our clean splits revealed its critical limitations: it performs decently on isolated flat notes but collapses when banknotes overlap.

⚠️ CRITICAL FAILURES: The base model is blind to partially covered banknote borders, overlapping notes, fanned stacks, and tilted smartphone angles.

How well does synthetic data transfer to real data?

01. Starting with 3D Images

At first, we generated basic 3D images of banknotes floating on a blank screen. It was an easy way to make lots of data. But when we tested the model on actual photos from a phone, it failed. The 3D images were too perfect, so the model got confused by real lighting, shadows, and messy backgrounds.

➔ ACCURACY FLOOR: 14.2%

02. Making It Look Real

To fix this, we made the 3D scenes look more like a real smartphone photo. We matched the camera angle, added shadows, and most importantly, threw in random "junk" objects behind the money. This taught the model to ignore background mess and focus only on the banknote. This simple trick was a huge success.

➔ ACCURACY JUMPED TO 50.3%

03. Combining Both Worlds

Since the 3D data was now working well, we used it to fix our biggest problem: messy piles of real cash. We mixed the tough 3D images with our real photos. The 3D data acted like a tutor, filling in the gaps where the real photos struggled. The result was a model that could read almost anything.

➔ HYBRID EVAL SCORE: 90.5%

// CURRENT LIMITATIONS

What Still Needs Work

[ERR_LIMIT_06]

Heavy Overlap

If most of a bill is hidden, the detector can easily miss it or mistakenly merge it with another bill.

Unsupported Bills

Rare or unsupported notes (like USD 2 or KHR 100,000) may be guessed as one of the known classes, causing estimation errors.

More Real Data

More real fanned and overlapping photos would help close the gap between demo scenes and real-world cash counts.

RIEL VISION IS A WORKING PROTOTYPE, NOT FINISHED.

// FINAL TAKEAWAY

The goal was a small local model that works better on the messy photos people actually take.

Real data gave the model the banknote appearance. Synthetic data helped create the fanned and overlapping cases we could not collect enough of. The final result is one compact browser model with honest strengths, visible limits, and a clear path for improvement.
